
# ⭐ Satellite Recycling Cost–Benefit Evaluation

(Top: English Version → Bottom: Chinese Version)

⸻

🇺🇸 1. Cost–Benefit Before Government Subsidy (No Subsidy Scenario)

1.1 Mission Costs (Per Recovery Mission)
	•	Launch cost for recovery spacecraft: $50–70M
	•	Mission operations, tracking, ground support: $5–15M
	•	Fuel resupply, robotic arm maintenance, risk insurance: $5–10M
	•	Development amortization of specialized “recycling spacecraft”:
	•	Estimated $200–500M spread across multiple missions

➡️ Total cost for one mission: approx. $80–120M

If each mission recovers 10 satellites,
	•	Cost per satellite = $8–12M
	•	Recovered material value = $3–8M (aluminum, panels, sensors)

1.2 Economic Result Without Subsidy
	•	Cost > Recovered Value
	•	Company suffers a deficit per satellite
	•	No commercial incentive
	•	Private companies will NOT do it voluntarily
	•	Only motivation: reduce debris (non-profit reason)

📌 Conclusion (Before Subsidy):
Economically negative. Technically possible but financially unattractive.

⸻

🇺🇸 2. Cost–Benefit After Government Subsidy (With Subsidy Scenario)

Assume government subsidizes 50–70% of mission cost
(common in aerospace R&D and national defense programs)

2.1 New Cost Structure (After Subsidy)

If a mission cost is $100M:
	•	Government pays $50–70M
	•	Private company pays $30–50M

If the mission still recovers 10 satellites:
	•	Actual private-sector cost per satellite ≈ $3–5M
	•	Recovered material value ≈ $3–8M

➡️ Now private companies can break even or gain a small profit.

2.2 Strategic National Benefits

Government subsidy also creates:
	•	National on-orbit servicing capability
	•	Control over orbital safety (debris mitigation)
	•	International influence (other countries pay for your service)
	•	Long-term industrial development (robotics, propulsion, space manufacturing)

📌 Conclusion (After Subsidy):
Economically viable + strategically valuable.
A new national industry becomes possible.

⸻

🇺🇸 3. Final Conclusion (English)

Without subsidy, satellite recycling is financially negative because recovery missions cost more than the material value obtained.
However, with government subsidy covering 50–70% of mission expenses, the cost to private companies drops below the value of the recovered materials. This turns satellite recycling from a loss-making operation into a viable commercial activity.

More importantly, subsidy-backed recycling grants the nation unique strategic advantages: debris reduction, orbital safety, in-orbit servicing capability, and international dependence on your country’s space infrastructure.

Thus, government-supported satellite recycling is not only economically feasible but also forms a critical component of long-term national security and industrial competitiveness.

🇨🇳 1. 補貼前：成本效益分析（無補貼情境）

1.1 任務成本（一次回收飛行）
	•	回收太空船發射：5,000–7,000 萬美元
	•	任務操作、地面控制、導航：500–1,500 萬美元
	•	補燃料、維修機械臂、保險：500–1,000 萬美元
	•	回收專用太空船研發攤提：
	•	2–5 億美元 分攤到多年任務

➡️ 單次任務總成本：約 8,000–12,000 萬美元

如果一次回收 10 顆衛星：
	•	每顆回收成本 = 約 800–1,200 萬美元
	•	可回收材料的價值 = 約 300–800 萬美元

1.2 無補貼的經濟結果
	•	成本 > 回收材料價值
	•	民間企業一定賠錢
	•	不會主動做
	•	只有「減少太空垃圾」這種公益理由才有人願意做

📌 補貼前結論：
商業不可行。技術可行、財務不可行。

⸻

🇨🇳 2. 補貼後：成本效益分析（有補貼情境）

假設政府補貼 50–70%（航太領域非常常見）

2.1 補貼後的成本結構

一趟任務 1 億美元：
	•	政府補貼 5,000–7,000 萬
	•	公司實付 3,000–5,000 萬

仍然回收 10 顆衛星：
	•	民間每顆成本 ≈ 300–500 萬美元
	•	材料價值 ≈ 300–800 萬美元

➡️ 民間公司開始 打平甚至微利

2.2 對國家的戰略收益
	•	建立 太空軌道維修能力
	•	控制 太空垃圾風險
	•	國際衛星運營商依賴你（付費）
	•	形成新產業（機械臂、推進器、航太製造）
	•	提升太空主權

📌 補貼後結論：
不只可行，還會成為重要的新產業與國家戰略資產。

⸻

🇨🇳 3. 最終結論（中文）

在沒有補貼的情況下，衛星回收的成本遠高於可回收材料的價值，是一門注定虧損的任務。
然而，一旦政府補貼 50–70%，民間企業的實際成本就會降低到可接受範圍，甚至低於回收材料的等效價值，使衛星回收從「賠錢」變成「可營利」。

更重要的是，政府補貼讓國家獲得太空軌道維修能力、降低太空垃圾風險，並建立在軌基礎設施，使其他國家的衛星必須依賴你。這將形成未來太空經濟的重要基礎。

因此：
政府支持下的衛星回收，不僅經濟可行，更是國家安全與太空主權的重要戰略項目。
