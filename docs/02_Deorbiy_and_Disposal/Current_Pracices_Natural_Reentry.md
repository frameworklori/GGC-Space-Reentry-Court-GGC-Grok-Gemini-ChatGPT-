# Current Practices: Natural Reentry and Burn-Up  
*(English Version — Public-Friendly Edition)*

---

## 1. What This Topic Is About
This document explains how satellites are currently disposed of at the end of their life.  
Today, the most common method is **natural reentry**, where satellites burn up in the atmosphere.

It is simple.  
It is cheap.  
But it creates long-lasting atmospheric pollution.

---

## 2. What Is Natural Reentry?
Most low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellites slowly lose altitude because of atmospheric drag.

When a satellite reaches about 80–100 km:
- It heats up to ~7,000°C  
- Most of its structure melts or vaporizes  
- Around 20–30% may survive as fragments  
- All aluminum turns into **Al₂O₃ particles**

Natural reentry = “letting the satellite fall and burn.”

This is currently the **default industry standard**.

---

## 3. Why Companies Prefer Natural Reentry
### ✔ Cheapest option  
No fuel required, no tug needed.

### ✔ Operationally simple  
Just shut down the satellite and wait.

### ✔ Considered safe “most of the time”  
Most fragments fall into the ocean.

But **cheap for the operator** does not mean **cheap for the planet**.

---

## 4. Problems With Natural Reentry

### ● 1. Long-lasting atmospheric pollution  
Each satellite produces ~30 kg of Al₂O₃ micro-particles.

These stay 5–10 years in the stratosphere.

### ● 2. Ozone layer impact  
Particles serve as surfaces that accelerate ozone destruction.

### ● 3. Uncontrolled fragment risks  
Even 1% survival means pieces can hit:
- Houses  
- Cars  
- People  
- Power lines  
- Aircraft routes  

### ● 4. Scaling problem  
One satellite is small pollution.  
Ten thousand satellites become a **global environmental issue**.

---

## 5. Current Industry Guidelines (Simple Summary)

- NASA and ESA require reentry within 25 years  
- No rules about pollution  
- No limits on how many satellites may burn up  
- No rules on debris falling on land  
- No reporting requirement for materials burned  

In short:  
**We only regulate “when” satellites fall, not “what they release.”**

---

## 6. What Scientists Recommend
Many researchers now argue for:

- Tracking reentry emissions  
- Replacing aluminum with safer materials  
- Using controlled reentry instead of natural burn-up  
- Using tugs or recycling for large satellites  
- Setting global pollution limits

# 目前做法：自然再入與燒蝕  
*(中文版本 — 大眾閱讀版)*

---

## 1. 這個主題在討論什麼  
本文件解釋目前業界最常使用的衞星處理方式：  
**自然再入（自然掉落 + 大氣燒蝕）**。

這種方式：

- 簡單  
- 便宜  
- 但會造成長期環境污染  

---

## 2. 什麼是自然再入？
大多數近地軌道（LEO）衞星會因大氣阻力逐漸下降。

當衞星下降到 80–100 公里時：

- 溫度上升到約 7,000°C  
- 結構融化、汽化  
- 約 20–30% 仍可能成為碎片  
- 鋁全部變成 **Al₂O₃ 微粒**

「自然再入」的意思就是：  
**讓衞星自己掉下來燒掉。**

這是目前的業界標準。

---

## 3. 為何企業偏好自然再入？

### ✔ 最便宜  
不用額外燃料，也不需要拖船。

### ✔ 最簡單  
關機，等它掉下來。

### ✔ 大多數碎片會掉入海洋  
被視為「大致安全」。

但：  
**對於企業便宜不代表對地球便宜。**

---

## 4. 自然再入的問題

### ● 1. 大氣長期污染  
每顆衞星會產生約 30 公斤 Al₂O₃ 微粒。

停留 5–10 年。

### ● 2. 影響臭氧層  
微粒提供臭氧破壞反應的平台。

### ● 3. 失控的碎片風險  
即使 1% 生還碎片，也可能砸到：

- 房屋  
- 車輛  
- 行人  
- 電力設施  
- 航空航線  

### ● 4. 星鏈化造成規模性問題  
一顆衞星污染小；  
一萬顆衞星＝全球環境危機。

---

## 5. 目前業界規範（簡化版）

- NASA/ESA 要求 25 年內再入  
- 沒有污染限制  
- 也沒有限制多少衞星可以燒毀  
- 沒有碎片落地地面責任規範  
- 不需要申報再入時燃燒了什麼材料  

簡單來說：  
**目前只有「再入時間規範」，沒有「再入污染規範」。**

---

## 6. 科學家的建議

- 監測再入排放量  
- 以替代材料取代鋁  
- 推行可控再入（而不是自然掉落）  
- 大型衞星使用拖船回收  
- 全球共同設定污染上限
